## Abstract
### Background
Cardiovascular disease is a general term for a group of conditions that affect the heart and blood vessels (NHS, 2022). It affects over half a billion people worldwide and accounts for 32% of global deaths (World Health Organization, 2025). Early interventions such as lifestyle changes and medication significantly reduce mortality and long term morbidity. Machine Learing offers the opportunite for early detection to help reduce number of deaths caused by cardiovascular disease.
## Methods and Reproducibility

## Results
Logistic Regression outperformed XGBoost across all metrics, achieving accuracy of 88.5%, AUC of 0.952 and recall of 0.96 for the disease class with only 1 false negative. XGBoost achieved accuracy of 86.9%, AUC of 0.948 and 3 false negatives. 10-fold cross-validation confirmed LR superiority (AUC 0.906 ± 0.049 vs 0.898 ± 0.046). Wilcoxon signed-rank testing indicated no statistically significant difference between models (p=0.375). thal, ca and cp were consistently identified as the strongest predictors across both models.

## Conclusion 
Logistic Regression outperformed XGBoost across all metrics despite being the simpler model, consistent with published literature showing complex models do not consistently outperform simpler alternatives on small clinical datasets. Learning curves confirmed XGBoost overfitting despite tuning. Both models achieved AUC of 0.95, placing results at the top end of published benchmarks. The lower false negative rate of LR is clinically significant in cardiac triage where missed diagnoses carry higher cost than false alarms. Given statistical equivalence and greater interpretability, LR is the preferred model for clinical deployment. Key limitations include small sample size, single institution origin, 1980s data collection, and a male-dominated cohort introducing sex bias.
